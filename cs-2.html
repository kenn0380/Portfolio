Client

US Department of Education

Role

UX / UI Designer

Brief

Redesign Dept. of Ed's Navigation and UI to adhere to UX best practices, UI Heuristics, and User Research findings

Tools

Discover Explore Develop Deliver

The US Department of Education is a nonprofit aimed information-based website aimed at informing users about current laws, grants, programs, and news regarding public education.

→ See ED's current website

Our Challenge

Restructure the ED website and navigation to allow users to more efficiently find the content they're looking for.

Hypothesis

Users will become lost and overwhelmed by the lack of structure and high volume of information on the ED website. We risk them using other sites through Google or their own institution, despite ED's content being more credible and up-to-date.

User Persona

User goals:

  • Support her kids, and help them follow their dreams
  • Avoid spending more money than is necessary on education
  • Being the source of truth when her kids come to her for help
→ User Persona

Usability Testing

I would never think to look at that under grants or that full list of programs.

In five usability tests only

56.2% of tasks

were fully accomplished.

  • In confirmation of our hypothesis, users struggled with goal-based tasks, such as "you want to find eligibility requirements for the Blue-Ribbon Program," becuase the content is located in the IA in unexpected places.
  • In other tasks, users commented that the organization of content on individual pages doesn't prioritize its relevance and relationship to other content chunks. For instance, a scholarship for studying abroad is prioritized above the fold on the main Grants page, and in the same text style as more important links, such as Pell Grants.

The Problem

The Dept. of ED website is designed to give clear, credible information to users about public education. We have observed that users cannot find the information they need to help accomplish their goals, because the UI and information architecture are not aligned with the user's mental model, and cause unnecessary excise. How might we restructure the website and UI so that usability results for the same tasks improve?

We observed in Heuristic Analyses and our Usability Tests, for example, that a lack of consistency and standards in the text styles and imagery causes users to doubt the relationship and relevance of similar components in the UI. Therefore, they often continue on a user flow which will not solve their goal.

→ Feature Proposition

Ideation & Feature Proposition

Our insight synthesis lead us to three feasible and viable solutions for the users' problems:

  • Restructured menu and navigation
  • Consistent Typography and text style hierarchy
  • Clean, objective micro-interactions

Mood Board

What worked for users:

  • Headers now contain breadcrumbs and titles consistent with the main navigation.

  • We selected imagery that had a stronger relationship with the content it represented.

What we changed:

  • Usability Testing participants expressed that the navigation photos took up too much space on mobile.

  • Users struggled to differentiate the utility bar from the main navigation. We iterated the compononet's fill color in the design system accordingly.
